# First Scarecrow Planning: Protect Crops Before They Disappear

Crows are easy to underestimate because early Spring starts small. A few parsnips feel safe, then the farm grows, one plant vanishes overnight, and the loss is hard to explain if you did not know crop protection had become part of the routine.

This guide helps new players decide when to build the first Scarecrow, where to place it, and how to expand crops without turning every morning into a repair job. The goal is not a perfect layout. The goal is a simple habit: protect the field before you scale it.

## Build Before The Field Gets Big

Small starter patches are less scary than large crop fields, but do not treat that as permission to ignore crows forever. Once you are planting beyond a tiny opening field, make crop protection part of the same plan as watering, seed buying, and pathing.

The practical move is to craft and place a Scarecrow before spending a major share of your gold on seeds. If the farm cannot protect the crop yet, shrink the planting plan until it can. A smaller protected field is usually better than a larger field where one missing plant breaks a harvest plan, bundle plan, or seed-money plan.

This advice fails if you delay Farming progress so long that you cannot craft the Scarecrow when the field expands. In that case, keep the crop count conservative, finish the next harvest, and protect the next planting instead of gambling on a big unprotected field.

## Put The First Scarecrow Where Your Work Already Happens

Your first Scarecrow should protect the crops you actually water every morning, not an imagined final layout. Place it near the center of the active field so the surrounding crop tiles are covered while still leaving room to walk and water.

Do not place it at the edge of a field just because that looks tidy. Edge placement often wastes protection outside the crop area and leaves future rows exposed. If you are unsure, keep the early field compact and easy to see. A simple square or rectangle around the Scarecrow is easier to maintain than scattered patches across the farm.

The failure mode is building the item and then expanding around it without checking coverage. When new rows are added, ask whether they are still near the Scarecrow or whether the farm has quietly become two fields. If it has become two fields, add protection before planting the second one heavily.

## Do Not Confuse Crop Loss With Watering Mistakes

When a plant disappears, review the cause before changing your whole plan. Crows can eat crops that are not protected, but a crop can also fall behind because it was missed during watering, planted late, blocked by layout, or simply not ready yet.

Use the next morning as a check. If a crop tile is empty where a plant used to be, look at Scarecrow coverage first. If the plant is still there but harvest timing feels wrong, review watering and planting date instead. These are different problems: Scarecrows protect against crows, while consistent watering keeps the crop growing.

This distinction matters because the fix is different. More Scarecrows do not solve missed watering, and more watering does not protect exposed crops overnight.

## Expand In Protected Blocks

As the farm grows, think in blocks rather than single seeds. Before planting a new block, decide three things: can you water it, can you walk through it, and is it protected? If any answer is no, fix the layout before buying more seeds.

Protected blocks also make season changes easier. When Spring ends or Summer starts, you can clear, replant, or resize one field at a time instead of trying to remember which scattered crops were safe. This is especially useful before sprinklers, when the same field must be watered manually every day.

Avoid expanding only because you have spare gold. The real cost of a field is not just seeds. It is morning time, energy, protection, and attention. If a new crop block makes you skip mining, fishing, errands, or tool plans, the farm may be growing faster than your routine can support.

## Keep Rarecrows And Later Upgrades In Perspective

Rarecrows and later Scarecrow options can be fun collection goals, but a beginner farm does not need to solve every decoration and coverage problem immediately. The ordinary Scarecrow is enough to teach the core habit: crops should be protected before they become expensive.

If you collect special Scarecrows later, treat them as layout tools after the basics are stable. Do not delay basic crop protection because you want a nicer-looking version from an event or later unlock. A plain Scarecrow protecting seeds today is more useful than a perfect decorative plan that arrives after crops have already been lost.

This also keeps the guide away from late-game layout pressure. The first goal is not maximum farm design. It is preventing avoidable losses while you learn how large a field your save can handle.

## A Simple First-Season Crop Protection Loop

Use this loop any time you are about to expand crops:

1. Count whether the new planting is still a small starter patch or a real field.
2. Craft or place a Scarecrow before the field becomes expensive to lose.
3. Keep the field compact enough that coverage is easy to reason about.
4. Add new crop blocks near existing protection, not randomly across the farm.
5. If a crop disappears, check Scarecrow coverage before blaming growth timing.
6. Recheck coverage at the start of each season or after a major field redesign.

This loop is intentionally simple. It protects early crop investment without requiring a perfect farm planner, gold modeling, or a late-game layout.
